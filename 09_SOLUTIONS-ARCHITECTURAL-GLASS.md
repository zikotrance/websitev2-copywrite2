## Block 2: Definition Block

## What Is Architectural Glass?

Architectural glass encompasses structural and decorative glass systems used in commercial building interiors and exteriors, including glass partitions, railings and balustrades, canopies and overhead glazing, and facade or curtain wall systems. Unlike standard flat glass products, architectural glass must meet engineered performance requirements for structural loads, thermal cycling, impact resistance, and building code compliance including ADA and IBC standards. HILO architectural glass solutions span these categories with fabricated-to-specification tempered, laminated, and insulated glass units available in thicknesses from 3/8" to 3/4" and beyond, all backed by a 5-year structural warranty covering glass panels, hardware, seals, and coatings.

## Block 14: FAQ Block

## Frequently Asked Questions About Architectural Glass

### What type of glass is required for overhead canopy installations?

All overhead glazing, including canopies and skylights, must use laminated safety glass as required by building codes. Laminated glass consists of two or more glass layers bonded with an interlayer — typically PVB or SGP — that holds the glass together if breakage occurs, preventing shards from falling. HILO specifies laminated glass for every canopy and overhead glazing application, regardless of height or location, because the consequences of glass failure in overhead positions are severe. The specific glass thickness and interlayer type are determined by engineering calculations based on the canopy span, design loads (snow, wind, and live loads), and local building code requirements. Our engineering team develops these calculations as part of the quoting process for overhead glazing projects.

### How does HILO ensure code compliance for glass railing systems?

Glass railing systems must meet specific load requirements defined by the International Building Code (IBC) and local jurisdictions. These requirements include uniform load tests, concentrated load tests, and in-fill load tests that simulate the forces a railing will experience in actual use. HILO engineers each railing system to meet or exceed these requirements by specifying appropriate glass thickness — typically 1/2" to 3/4" tempered or laminated — selecting mounting hardware rated for the calculated loads, and designing attachment details that transfer loads properly to the building structure. For projects requiring ADA compliance, we ensure that handrail heights, extensions, and profiles meet accessibility standards. Engineering documentation including load calculations and, where required, PE-stamped drawings accompanies every railing system order.

### What is the lead time for architectural glass compared to other HILO products?

Architectural glass has a longer standard lead time than HILO's mirror and shower glass products. Standard architectural glass configurations ship within 4-8 weeks from order confirmation, compared to 2-4 weeks for catalogue shower glass and mirrors. Custom architectural glass requiring engineering review, PE-stamped drawings, or non-standard specifications may require 6-12 weeks depending on complexity. This longer timeline reflects the engineering requirements, fabrication processes, and quality verification steps involved in structural glass systems. Tempering, laminating, and insulated glass unit assembly each add production stages that mirror products do not require. We recommend engaging HILO early in the design process — ideally during schematic design or early construction documents — to allow adequate time for engineering review and fabrication without impacting your project schedule.

### Does HILO provide engineering support for custom facade systems?

Yes, HILO provides engineering support for custom facade and curtain wall projects. Our engineering capabilities include structural analysis of glass panels under wind and thermal loads, thermal performance modeling for insulated glass unit specifications, condensation resistance analysis for climate-specific requirements, and coordination with facade contractors and building envelope engineers. For projects requiring PE-stamped drawings, we work with licensed professional engineers who stamp documents compliant with local jurisdiction requirements. Custom facade systems require a collaborative design process where HILO's engineering team works alongside your architects and structural engineers to develop specifications that balance performance, aesthetics, and budget. Contact our commercial team early in your project to initiate this collaborative process.

### What is the difference between tempered and laminated architectural glass?

Tempered glass is heat-treated to create a surface compression that makes it four to five times stronger than annealed glass of the same thickness. When tempered glass breaks, it shatters into small, relatively harmless granular pieces rather than dangerous shards. Laminated glass consists of two or more glass layers bonded with a durable plastic interlayer that holds the glass together when broken, preventing it from falling or creating an opening. In architectural applications, tempered glass is commonly used for partitions and railings where breakage safety is the primary concern. Laminated glass is required for overhead applications such as canopies, and is preferred for facades where post-breakage integrity, sound attenuation, or security are important. HILO recommends the appropriate glass type for each application based on code requirements, performance needs, and project-specific conditions.
