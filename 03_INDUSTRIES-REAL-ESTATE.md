## Why Mirror Procurement Breaks Multi-Family Construction Schedules

Multi-family development operates on razor-thin margins where every procurement decision is measured in cost per unit, and glass products represent a deceptively large line item when multiplied across hundreds of identical bathrooms. When you are building 240 condominiums and each unit requires a mirror, a medicine cabinet, and a shower enclosure, even a modest per-unit saving compounds into thousands of dollars in direct savings — but only if your supplier can actually deliver that price at volume without substituting lower-quality products to protect their own margins. The temptation to source glass from the lowest bidder often backfires: commodity mirrors with inferior silvering begin to degrade within 18 months of occupancy, generating warranty callbacks that cost $150-300 each in labor and materials. For developers who offer buyer warranties at close-out, mirror failures during the warranty period become a direct hit to your customer service budget and your reputation in a competitive market where buyers compare developments obsessively online.

Phased delivery across construction milestones is where most glass suppliers fail multi-family developers, and the consequences cascade through your entire finishing schedule. A three-building, 240-unit project does not receive all glass products on a single day — mirrors for Building A are needed when its first 12 floors reach the finish stage, while Building B may not need its mirrors for another six weeks, and Building C's common area glass is a full quarter away from installation readiness. Coordinating these staggered deliveries across multiple buildings, multiple product types, and a construction schedule that shifts based on weather, labor availability, and permit timing requires a supplier with dedicated logistics management rather than a distributor who ships everything from a warehouse and hopes for the best. Missed delivery windows create cascading delays that idle your most expensive finishing trades and push your certificate of occupancy dates further into the future.

Buyer perception at the handover walkthrough creates pressure that many developers underestimate until they are facing deficiency lists and social media complaints. When a purchaser walks into their new condominium for the first time, the bathroom mirror is among the most scrutinized elements — it is large, reflective, and positioned at eye level where every imperfection is immediately visible. A mirror with edge defects, inconsistent LED lighting, or an off-brand appearance lowers the buyer's confidence in the overall build quality regardless of structural excellence. In competitive markets where buyers choose between multiple new-construction projects, these finish-level details influence purchase decisions, deficiency claim volumes, and post-close satisfaction scores that feed directly into your Net Promoter Score and your ability to sell remaining inventory at target prices.
